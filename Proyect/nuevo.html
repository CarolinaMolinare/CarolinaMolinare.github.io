Analysis of Fertility Rates in Chile

How has the fertility crisis evolved in Chile?

Chile is facing an unprecedented demographic crisis. In 2024, the country’s fertility rate fell to just 1.03 births per woman—one of the lowest levels observed globally. This is far below the replacement level of 2.1 needed to maintain a stable population, and it signals a structural shift in Chilean society with long-run implications for ageing, labour supply, and social protection systems.

But how did we get here? And what does this crisis look like when we zoom in across different dimensions—over time, across age groups, across regions within Chile, and in comparison to international benchmarks?

International context

Fertility decline is not new in Chile, but the pace of the recent drop is striking. This chart pulls the total fertility rate series directly from the World Bank’s WDI API (1960–2023), so the visualization is reproducible and automatically linked to the underlying source. Chile is shown against World Bank income-group aggregates, which makes the comparison intuitive: while fertility falls across all groups, Chile’s decline is steeper than the typical upper-middle-income trajectory, pushing it toward the very low levels usually associated with high-income OECD economies. Hovering over a year reveals the exact values for all groups in a single consolidated tooltip.

To show how severe Chile’s situation is in 2023, fertility rates are benchmarked against other OECD countries. While Chile already has preliminary data for 2024, year 2023 is used here because it is the latest year that is broadly comparable across the full set of OECD members. The data come directly from the World Bank’s WDI API, which makes the chart fully reproducible from the original source.

Chile stands out for the wrong reasons. Most OECD countries are above Chile’s level, and Chile sits near the bottom of the distribution. South Korea is the clear extreme case, but Chile is still among the lowest-fertility countries in the OECD. In other words, this is not simply a regional pattern—Chile is an outlier even within a group of high-income economies.

Who is (and isn't) having children?

Using Chilean birth records from the National Institute of Statistics (INE), this interactive calculator is designed to capture changes that are hard to read precisely from a standard line chart. In the early 1990s, births were concentrated at younger ages—especially 20–24 Instead of estimating differences by eye (or calculating them manually), the tool returns the exact percentage change for any age group and any pair of years, computed as (end − start) / start × 100.

The figure is built by combining two pieces developed in Google Colab: a multi-series line chart for context and a separate “calculator panel” that performs the year-to-year lookup and calculation. These components are then stitched together into a single interactive view so the selections update both the highlighted line and the calculated result.

This pattern suggests that the fertility decline is not uniform across the life cycle: it is concentrated in younger cohorts, whereas births at older ages fall less dramatically.

This chart complements the age-specific rates by showing how the distribution of births has shifted across age groups. By 2024, the centre of the distribution has moved upward, with 30–34 accounting for the largest share. This is not “postponement only”: the scale of the decline at younger ages is not fully offset at older ages, consistent with an overall reduction in births.

The percentages shown here were constructed in the underlying dataset before visualisation. In Vega-Lite, the data are then reshaped with a fold transform (turning age-group columns into a single “Age Group” field), and the stacked bars are normalised so each year sums to 100%, making changes in composition comparable over time.

Fertility trends also have a spatial dimension. The map shows the number of births across Chilean regions by year.

The Región Metropolitana (Santiago) dominates the distribution. Because its birth counts are an order of magnitude higher than most other regions, it is displayed using a separate blue scale; the rest of the country is shown on a green scale so regional variation remains visible rather than being compressed into a single colour.

The slider reveals a key point: births are declining across every region. This is not only a Santiago phenomenon or an urban–rural divide—the crisis is national.

Technically, the main challenge was implementing two independent colour scales (blue for Metropolitana, green for the rest) while keeping a single year slider that controls both layers simultaneously. A KPI box overlays the map to display national totals, styled with rounded corners and green accents for visual consistency with the rest of the project.

Closing remarks

Chile's fertility crisis is structural, sustained, and accelerating. It's not confined to one region or one age group. Women are having children later, but they're also having fewer of them overall. Compared to international peers, Chile stands as an extreme case. The policy implications are significant. Low fertility rates lead to an aging population, fewer workers supporting more retirees, and potential economic stagnation. Understanding how this crisis has evolved—across time, space, and demographics—is the first step toward designing evidence-based responses. The data and code for this project are publicly available on GitHub, with automated API calls ensuring the visualizations can be updated as new data becomes available. All charts are built using Vega-Lite and Altair, chosen for their declarative grammar and interactivity capabilities—essential for letting users explore these patterns themselves.
