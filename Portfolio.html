Section 3: Using a visualisation in policy commentary

Should Chile expand public funding for assisted reproduction to address very low fertility and a rapid shift of births toward older maternal ages? Chile sits near the bottom of the OECD fertility distribution. Births have also shifted to older ages: by 2024, over half will occur in women aged 30 and above, with the largest group being those aged 30–34. This is directly relevant to funding assisted reproduction, since it targets fertility constraints that become more salient at older maternal ages, where pregnancy is more risky.

Section 4: Re-creating someone else's chart

This is the graph that Chilean ex-president Sebastian Piñera shows during an official debate for the presidency about delinquency in Chile. He used it to argue that, under his previous administration (2010-2013), the crime rate was lower than in other presidential terms. However, the figure is a clear example of misleading data visualization: it manipulates the scale to exaggerate differences and make a modest variation appear disproportionately large. The second graph shows how the same data should be displayed with an appropriate scale—an approach that would have been far less supportive of Piñera's narrative. He ultimately won that election.

The first key change is to show a fully comparable series and clarify that the indicator measures victimization (not delinquency). Importantly, the victimization methodology was updated in 2016, which means the 2010 figure shown by Piñera comes from the old methodology and should not be plotted or compared as if it were equivalent. Using the consistent post-2016 series, the victimization rate varies only modestly over the period.

Section 5: Accessing data

5.1 API task

World Bank Indicators API (V2) follows this structure: https://api.worldbank.org/v2/country/{countries}/indicator/{indicator}?{parameters}

Where:
- Countries: lic (Low income countries); lmc (Lower middle income countries); mic (Middle income countries); umc (Upper middle income countries); hic (High income countries)
- Indicator: SP.DYN.TFRT.IN (Fertility rate, total (births per woman))
- Parameters: format=json (Returns data in JSON format); per_page=1000; date=1960:2023

API used: https://api.worldbank.org/v2/country/lic;lmc;mic;umc;hic/indicator/SP.DYN.TFRT.IN?format=json&per_page=1000&date=1960:2023

Section 8: Extracting a story from millions of prices

This section analyze the price evolution of the components of a typical English breakfast with data work in Colab
Chart 1 tracks the average prices of seven English breakfast items from 1995 to 2025, using LRPD data, and reveals significant inflation, particularly after 2022, in products such as bacon and sausage.

Section 9: Interactive charts

These two interactive charts go beyond basic tooltips by letting the user actively explore the data. The first is an interactive regional map of Chilean births (2010–2022) controlled by a year slider, which updates the choropleth in real time and reveals that births are declining across every region, not only in Metropolitana.

The second is an age-specific fertility “calculator” that combines a multi-series line chart with a linked calculator panel: users select an age group and two years, and the panel returns the exact percentage change, while simultaneously highlighting the corresponding series.

Section 10: Advanced Analysis and Machine Learning

10.1 Advanced Analysis

This advanced analytics chart applies a trend (de-trending) and shock-style comparison to the Emergency Contraception Pill to assess whether the policy coincides with a meaningful deviation from the underlying trajectory. The key finding is that the only group showing a clear shift consistent with the policy is women aged 15–19; other female age groups do not display a comparable break.

10.2 Machine Learning

The analysis conducts a supervised machine-learning regression to predict countries’ fertility rates using World Bank WDI data for 2019. After fetching and merging indicators with wbgapi, the sample is restricted to non-aggregate economies, and missing observations are dropped. GDP per capita (PPP) is then log-transformed.

A standardised feature matrix X is then constructed using female labour-force participation, log GDP per capita, and female upper-secondary completion, alongside a standardised target vector y for the fertility rate.

The model is estimated with sklearn’s LinearRegression, evaluated via cross-validation (R²), and used to generate out-of-fold predictions. The results are presented in an Altair scatter plot comparing actual versus predicted fertility, along with a residual-based uncertainty band.
